Strategic Rationale — Why This Structure

  • AI is automating Tier-1 SOC triage — job postings for basic security analysts have fallen ~53% since 2022. Jordan needs analysts who think above the automation layer, not just clear tickets.
  • One umbrella, two specializations — market all grads as Cybersecurity Analyst (SOC/IR) with a specialization line. JDs in Amman skew to SOC/IR generalists; specialization depth closes the deal.
  • Purple-team fluency is the differentiator — organizations are moving to fusion teams. Graduates who have operated both sides earn a premium and are harder to automate away.
  • DFIR demand tied to compliance pressure — "incident response" framing resonates with Amman banks, telcos, and government entities facing NIS2-aligned mandates.
  • AI skills are table stakes in 2026 — validated AI workflows (audit logs, hallucination checks, evidence-safe pipelines) embedded as a cross-cutting professional habit from Week 1.
  • 3-week shared phase is intentional — OS, networking, CLI, ethics, RoE, and the full evidence-safe foundation cannot be done responsibly in 2 weeks. Three weeks builds the habits that protect the program's legal defensibility and employer credibility for both tracks.

Cybersecurity Track — Wks 3–12 Spine (75 Learners)

  • Wk 3–4: Recon Pt 1 & 2 — Nmap/Masscan, OSINT, asset catalog, vuln sweep, attack surface map, passive/active dossier
  • Wk 5: Web attacks — OWASP Top 10, SQLi/XSS, Burp + SQLmap, PoC write-ups with fix-ready remediation
  • Wk 6–7: Exploitation & automation — Metasploit safe chains, Python tool-chaining, AI-assisted variants with mandatory audit log & hallucination check
  • Wk 7: Passwords & social engineering — Hashcat workflow, Gophish campaign, ethics-gated RoE, defense recs
  • Wk 8–9: AD attack paths Pt 1 & 2 — BloodHound/SharpHound, graph analysis, safe priv-esc demo, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, mitigation write-up
  • Wk 10: Priv-esc / EDR evasion — LotL techniques, detected vs. undetected log, EDR visibility & evasion trade-offs
  • Wk 11: C2 & OPSEC — Sliver/Mythic minimal deployment, operator SOPs, teardown checklist, blue-team review
  • Wk 12: Detection-aware offense — Sysmon enabled, logs shipped to ELK/Wazuh, "what to monitor" list per TTP, purple reporting Pt 1 draft
Every red week — mandatory: "Write a detection" task. Learners propose one SIEM rule or Sigma detection for the technique they just used. Non-negotiable. Red graduates who cannot think defensively are harder to place and more likely to cause harm.

Digital Forensics Track — Wks 3–12 Spine (25 Learners)

  • Wk 3: Chain of custody & imaging deep-dive — full SOP, write-blocker practice on shared physical kit, complete hash validation workflow, ESD + no-boot handling, smartphone awareness. Builds on Wk 1 intro.
  • Wk 4: Windows artifacts — Registry, top Event IDs cheat sheet, timeline stitching; live cross-track exercise: analyze artifacts from Red track's Wk 3–4 recon scans
  • Wk 5: Linux / macOS artifacts — cross-OS correlation workbook, unified logs, plists, macOS Sonoma TCC deltas
  • Wk 6: Unified timelines & rapid triage — Plaso + Timesketch, triage vs. full acquisition, manifest + error budgets, threat intel workflow integration
  • Wk 7–8: AI-assisted triage pipeline Pt 1 & 2 — local/offline LLMs only; derived artifacts only (never raw evidence to AI); JSON/YAML audit log (model/prompt/version/variance); legal admissibility; validation & governance
  • Wk 9: Memory forensics — Volatility triage, profile selection, process tree correlation, memory triage report
  • Wk 10: Malware analysis — static triage (hashes/PE headers/strings), sandbox awareness, memory-to-process-tree correlation, defensive recommendations
  • Wk 11: Mobile forensics — AFU/BFU decision tree, ALEAPP/ILEAPP, privacy/scope Go/No-Go memo. Shared physical kit required.
  • Wk 12: VM & cloud artifacts — snapshot extraction, SaaS/IaaS audit-log correlation (Okta/AAD/M365), privacy boundaries & retention policy
Physical kit access (Wks 3 & 11): Publish the rotation schedule at Week 0. 25 kits serve 25 DFIR learners — one per learner during these two sessions, eliminating wait time. These are the only weeks where virtual infrastructure cannot substitute for real hardware.

AI Integration — Cross-Cutting Layer

  • Not a module — a professional habit. Every week has an AI touchpoint with a mandatory audit trail, starting Week 1.
  • Red track: AI co-pilot for payload variant generation (logged + validated before use); AI-assisted report drafting; hallucination check required before any submission
  • DFIR track: Local/offline LLMs only; derived artifacts only — never raw evidence to AI; JSON/YAML log captures model, prompt, version, and output variance every use
  • Both tracks: AI governance policy in capstone RoE; AI risk assessment required in final deliverable
  • AI threat awareness (Wks 6–7): Deepfake social engineering, AI-optimized phishing, model theft vectors woven into exploitation labs
  • Career framing: Tier-1 triage is being automated. Graduates are the people who oversee, validate, and tune AI tools — this is taught explicitly, not implied.

Lab Environment — Recommendation & Cost Decision

VERDICT: Rotational Hybrid Model — Keep Current Classroom Footprint, Prioritize DFIR for Physical Labs, Run Cybersecurity as Hybrid

The program does not require bigger classrooms if delivery is structured around rotation instead of full-cohort physical attendance. The correct model is to keep concept sessions virtual for all learners, reserve in-person space for the highest-value lab work, and treat physical access as a scheduled resource. Digital Forensics gets priority access when hardware and evidence-handling are essential; Cybersecurity is delivered hybrid, with rotating in-person lab access and virtual-first execution for work that does not depend on physical equipment.

✓ Classroom Strategy — Use Existing Space More Intelligently
  • · Sunday concept: fully virtual for all learners — removes the largest space bottleneck immediately
  • · Mon/Tue and Wed/Thu: keep the current all-day in-person lab structure, but assign space by rotating group and track priority
  • · No larger room required if only one group is physically present at a time and all groups remain on the same instructional week
  • · Cybersecurity groups: rotate through in-person lab weeks; remote groups complete the same lab objectives with TA support and artifact-based checkpoints
  • · Thursday: use for virtual reporting, purple-team comparison, demos, and cross-track synthesis when space is constrained
  • → Space constraint solved operationally, not through real-estate expansion
◎ Track Priority — Physical for DFIR, Hybrid for Cybersecurity
  • · DFIR (25 learners): priority use of physical classroom and kit access during hardware-dependent weeks, especially Wk 3 imaging and Wk 11 mobile forensics
  • · Cybersecurity (75 learners): primarily virtual-first labs, with rotating in-person attendance for high-friction debugging, demonstrations, and purple-team work
  • · Physical kits: maintain 25 DFIR kits — one per DFIR learner during required hardware sessions, no waiting or station-sharing
  • · Remote cyber weeks: not passive or recorded-only; they are structured lab weeks with TA office hours, required evidence, and the same deliverables as in-person groups
  • · Key principle: physical presence is reserved for work that genuinely benefits from shared space; everything else should scale virtually
  • → DFIR gets the room when the room matters most; Cybersecurity scales without sacrificing rigor
Operating implication: keep all groups synchronized by week, rotate in-person access instead of creating different pacing tracks, and publish the classroom / kit schedule before launch. The biggest requirement is not more space — it is tighter operational coordination, stronger TA support, and artifact-based lab design that works in both in-person and remote modes.

Staffing & Assessment Model

  • Wks 0–2 (Shared): Lead instructor × 1 + support × 1. 4h curriculum dev + 2h teaching + 1h sync/week. Both instructors present for Wk 2 Red/Blue debrief — it requires two facilitators.
  • Wks 3–12 (Split): Red instructor + DFIR instructor run parallel tracks. 4h dev + 2h teach + 1h sync each/week. Thursday sessions rotate joint — instructors alternate hosting.
  • Wks 13–16 (Converge): Both instructors joint for all sessions. 6–8h/week + grading. CISO panel (Wk 16) should include 2–3 external Jordan-based practitioners.
  • Guest sessions (Wks 5, 9, 13): Jordan SOC/DFIR practitioners, lunch-and-learn format. Both tracks attend — builds realistic job expectations and cross-track professional networks.

Assessment framework:

  • Weekly readiness checks — both tracks, shared low-stakes rubric
  • Track-specific lab rubrics — Wks 3–12, graded on technical accuracy + documentation quality
  • Purple labs (Wks 13–14) — graded equally on attack realism and detection quality
  • Capstone (Wks 15–16) — joint briefing quality, technical artifacts, AI governance compliance

Risk Mitigation & Critical Flags

  • Evidence habits in Wk 1, not Wk 3: Chain-of-custody, hashing, and time-normalization are in the shared foundation. If DFIR learners miss this during an offense-heavy opening, the program's legal defensibility is at risk from the very first imaging lab.
  • "Write a detection" every red week: Non-negotiable. Red graduates who cannot think defensively are harder to place and more likely to cause harm in real engagements.
  • AI audit log from Week 1: Every AI-assisted output logged (model/prompt/version/variance). This is a hire-or-no-hire signal in AI-augmented SOCs by 2026.
  • Broadband risk in Amman: Offline VM image library downloaded at Week 0 is the fallback. No lab session should ever be blocked by a connectivity failure.
  • Physical kit schedule at Week 0: DFIR learners need guaranteed access in Wk 3 (imaging) and Wk 11 (mobile). Ambiguity here causes the highest-stakes labs to fail.
  • Honest learner framing: AI is automating Tier-1 triage. This program's emphasis on analysis, decision-making, and AI governance is the commercially correct response — communicate it as a feature, not a warning.
