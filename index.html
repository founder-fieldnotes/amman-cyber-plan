Program architecture

A sequence designed for execution, not just instruction

The structure creates a common security language first, then specialized skill depth, then cross-track pressure testing in a purple-team and capstone context.
Phase 1
Wks 0–2

Shared foundation

Joint cohort. Evidence-safe habits, OS/networking fluency, VM setup, scripting, and the Week 2 Red/Blue feedback loop.

Phase 2
Wks 3–12

Diverge — specialty tracks

Split labs with shared scenario logic. Cybersecurity focuses on offensive workflows and defensive translation; DFIR focuses on evidence, artifacts, timelines, and triage.

Phase 3
Wks 13–16

Converge — purple + capstone

Joint exercises, executive reporting, and investor-credible final artifacts. Every learner finishes with both technical and decision-quality evidence.

Why this structure works

Strategic rationale

  • AI is shrinking basic triage work. Jordan needs analysts who can think above the automation layer, not just clear tickets.
  • One umbrella, two specializations. Graduates are marketable as Cybersecurity Analysts (SOC/IR), with specialization depth that helps close placement.
  • Purple-team fluency is a differentiator. Employers increasingly value analysts who understand both attack paths and detection logic.
  • DFIR demand maps well to compliance pressure. Incident response framing lands with banks, telcos, and government environments.
  • AI skills are now baseline professional behavior. Audit logs, validation, and evidence-safe AI usage are not a bonus topic; they are required practice.
  • The shared phase is not optional. Without common habits in documentation, evidence, and security reasoning, both tracks weaken.

Track design

Two tracks, one coherent hiring story

Both tracks operate under one market-facing identity while preserving real specialization depth in the middle of the program.
Cybersecurity track · 75 learners

Offense with defensive accountability

  • Wks 3–4: Recon Pt 1 & 2 — Nmap, Masscan, OSINT, asset catalog, vuln sweep, attack surface map
  • Wk 5: Web attacks — OWASP, SQLi/XSS, Burp + SQLmap, remediation-ready reporting
  • Wks 6–7: Exploitation & automation — Metasploit chains, Python tool chaining, logged AI-assisted workflow
  • Wk 7: Passwords & social engineering — Hashcat, Gophish, ethics-gated ROE
  • Wks 8–9: AD attack paths — BloodHound, graph analysis, mitigation mapping
  • Wk 10: Priv-esc / EDR evasion — LotL tradeoffs, visibility, detected vs undetected paths
  • Wk 11: C2 & OPSEC — minimal Sliver/Mythic, teardown discipline, Blue-team review
  • Wk 12: Detection-aware offense — Sysmon, ELK/Wazuh mapping, “what to monitor” list per TTP
Non-negotiable: every Red week includes a “write a detection” task.
Security+ CySA+ CASP+ MITRE ATT&CK
Digital forensics track · 25 learners

Evidence, timelines, and defensible analysis

  • Wk 3: Chain of custody & imaging deep-dive — full SOP, write blocker practice, hash validation, no-boot handling
  • Wk 4: Windows artifacts — registry, event IDs, timeline stitching, Red recon artifact analysis
  • Wk 5: Linux / macOS artifacts — unified logs, plists, cross-OS correlation
  • Wk 6: Unified timelines & rapid triage — Plaso, Timesketch, manifest discipline
  • Wks 7–8: AI-assisted triage pipeline — local/offline only, derived artifacts only, legal admissibility and validation
  • Wk 9: Memory forensics — Volatility, process tree correlation, triage report
  • Wk 10: Malware analysis — static triage, sandbox awareness, defensive recommendations
  • Wk 11: Mobile forensics — AFU/BFU decision tree, ALEAPP/ILEAPP, privacy and scope memo
  • Wk 12: VM & cloud artifacts — audit-log correlation, privacy boundaries, retention logic
Physical kit access (Wks 3 & 11): the DFIR hardware setup is intentionally lean and purpose-built. It is not a broad sysadmin toolkit. Priority goes to enough RAM and SSD capacity for VM-heavy work, plus the few physical items required for evidence-safe imaging and mobile forensics. Hardware write blockers, removable media, and a limited set of mobile devices matter more than extra networking accessories. Optional router or networking gear should be treated as backup equipment, not standard kit contents.

Operating model

A layout designed to support good decisions

These are the three blocks investors and program operators need to understand together: the delivery model, the cost model, and the staffing model.
Decision

Rotational hybrid keeps the current room footprint viable

The program does not require bigger classrooms if delivery is structured around scheduled hybrid rotation instead of full-cohort physical attendance. Concept sessions remain virtual for all learners, the existing room is reserved for the highest-value in-person lab moments, and all three groups participate in both formats across the program.

Classroom strategy
  • Sunday concept: fully virtual for all learners
  • Mon–Thu labs: hybrid all day, with controlled physical attendance
  • All three groups are hybrid: no fully remote group
  • Thursday: purple-team comparison, demos, and reporting anchor
  • All groups stay synchronized by week
Track priority
  • DFIR: priority access during hardware-dependent weeks
  • Cybersecurity: rotates in-person participation for guided labs, debugging, and purple work
  • Online participation: synchronous, structured, and artifact-driven
  • Physical presence is scheduled, not assumed
Bottom line: the main challenge is not real estate. It is hybrid orchestration, group synchronization, and disciplined operational planning.
Lab infrastructure — cost model

Lean, reusable, and aligned to the real training need

  • Virtual / cloud lab stack: approximately $10–15 per learner per month for core range access, shared infrastructure, and platform costs
  • Monthly virtual cost: approximately $1,000–1,500 across the full cohort of 100 learners
  • 4-month cohort virtual cost: approximately $4,000–6,000
  • DFIR shared hardware kit (25 total): lean, purpose-built kit focused on imaging and mobile forensics rather than a full sysadmin or networking toolkit
  • Target hardware priority: sufficient RAM and SSD capacity for VM-heavy workflows, plus write blockers, removable media, and limited mobile devices for DFIR lab use
  • Estimated DFIR hardware cost: approximately $125–200 per kit = roughly $3,125–5,000 one-time, depending on how much existing equipment can be reused
  • Wireless routers and extra networking accessories: optional backup equipment only, not standard per-kit requirements
  • Additional classroom cost: none required if the current room is used as a rotational hybrid space
  • Future cohorts: recurring cost drops to the virtual stack plus minor replenishment / replacement of shared hardware
Total Cohort 1 infrastructure cost: approximately $7,125–11,000
Future cohort cost: approximately $4,000–6,000 once shared hardware is already owned
This version assumes the advisor’s recommendation: no unnecessary sysadmin toolkit, no default networking bundle, and hardware spending prioritized toward the VM and DFIR workflows that actually matter instructionally.
Staffing & assessment

Built for parity across in-room and online learners

  • Core delivery: 1 virtual concept session + hybrid labs across 3 groups
  • Wks 0–2: Lead instructor ×1, support instructor ×1, hybrid TA support
  • Wks 3–12: Cyber lead ×1, DFIR lead ×1, minimum 3 TAs, preferred 4–5 TAs
  • Wks 13–16: both instructors joint for purple and capstone delivery
  • Hybrid facilitation rule: one person owns the room, one owns the online experience
  • Assessment: identical deliverables and grading standards regardless of participation mode
  • Primary grading signal: artifacts, readiness checks, rubric-scored labs, capstone reporting, and AI governance compliance
