Strategic Rationale — Why This Structure

  • AI is automating Tier-1 SOC triage — job postings for basic security analysts have fallen ~53% since 2022. Jordan needs analysts who think above the automation layer, not just clear tickets.
  • One umbrella, two specializations — market all grads as Cybersecurity Analyst (SOC/IR) with a specialization line. JDs in Amman skew to SOC/IR generalists; specialization depth closes the deal.
  • Purple-team fluency is the differentiator — organizations are moving to fusion teams. Graduates who have operated both sides earn a premium and are harder to automate away.
  • DFIR demand tied to compliance pressure — "incident response" framing resonates with Amman banks, telcos, and government entities facing NIS2-aligned mandates.
  • AI skills are table stakes in 2026 — validated AI workflows (audit logs, hallucination checks, evidence-safe pipelines) embedded as a cross-cutting professional habit from Week 1.
  • 3-week shared phase is intentional — OS, networking, CLI, ethics, RoE, and the full evidence-safe foundation cannot be done responsibly in 2 weeks. Three weeks builds the habits that protect the program's legal defensibility and employer credibility for both tracks.

  • Wk 6–7: Exploitation & automation — Metasploit safe chains, Python tool-chaining, AI-assisted variants with mandatory audit log & hallucination check

Digital Forensics Track — Wks 3–12 Spine (25 Learners)

  • Wk 3: Chain of custody & imaging deep-dive — full SOP, write-blocker practice on shared physical kit, complete hash validation workflow, ESD + no-boot handling, smartphone awareness. Builds on Wk 1 intro.
  • Wk 4: Windows artifacts — Registry, top Event IDs cheat sheet, timeline stitching; live cross-track exercise: analyze artifacts from Red track's Wk 3–4 recon scans

  • Wk 11: Mobile forensics — AFU/BFU decision tree, ALEAPP/ILEAPP, privacy/scope Go/No-Go memo. Shared physical kit required.
  • Wk 12: VM & cloud artifacts — snapshot extraction, SaaS/IaaS audit-log correlation (Okta/AAD/M365), privacy boundaries & retention policy

AI Integration — Cross-Cutting Layer

  • Not a module — a professional habit. Every week has an AI touchpoint with a mandatory audit trail, starting Week 1.
  • Red track: AI co-pilot for payload variant generation (logged + validated before use); AI-assisted report drafting; hallucination check required before any submission
  • DFIR track: Local/offline LLMs only; derived artifacts only — never raw evidence to AI; JSON/YAML log captures model, prompt, version, and output variance every use
  • Both tracks: AI governance policy in capstone RoE; AI risk assessment required in final deliverable
  • AI threat awareness (Wks 6–7): Deepfake social engineering, AI-optimized phishing, model theft vectors woven into exploitation labs
  • Career framing: Tier-1 triage is being automated. Graduates are the people who oversee, validate, and tune AI tools — this is taught explicitly, not implied.

Lab Environment — Recommendation & Cost Decision

VERDICT: Rotational Hybrid Model — Keep Current Classroom Footprint, Run All Three Groups as Hybrid, Prioritize DFIR for Hardware-Dependent Labs

The program does not require bigger classrooms if delivery is structured around scheduled hybrid rotation instead of full-cohort physical attendance. The correct model is to keep concept sessions virtual for all learners, use the existing classroom for high-value in-person lab time, and ensure that all three groups have both in-person and online participation across the program. Digital Forensics gets priority access when hardware and evidence-handling are essential; Cybersecurity also uses the physical room on a rotating basis for guided labs, debugging, demonstrations, and purple-team work.

✓ Classroom Strategy — Hybrid for All Three Groups
  • · Sunday concept: fully virtual for all learners — removes the largest space bottleneck immediately
  • · Mon/Tue and Wed/Thu: keep the current all-day lab structure, but rotate which learners are physically present while the rest of the same group joins online
  • · All three groups are hybrid: every group has scheduled in-person time and scheduled online participation; no group is remote-only
  • · No larger room required if physical attendance is capped, coordinated, and tied to the highest-value lab moments
  • · Thursday: use for virtual reporting, purple-team comparison, demos, and cross-track synthesis when space is constrained
  • → Space constraint solved through rotational hybrid delivery, not real-estate expansion
◎ Track Priority — Physical First for DFIR, Scheduled Physical Access for Cybersecurity
  • · DFIR (25 learners): priority use of physical classroom and kit access during hardware-dependent weeks, especially Wk 3 imaging and Wk 11 mobile forensics
  • · Cybersecurity (75 learners): hybrid every week — some learners in-room, others online, with rotation so all experience guided in-person support over time
  • · Physical kits: maintain 25 DFIR kits — one per DFIR learner during required hardware sessions, no waiting or station-sharing
  • · Online participation: structured, synchronous, and artifact-driven — not passive or recorded-only
  • · Key principle: every learner gets both formats; physical presence is reserved for work that benefits most from shared space, instructor proximity, and hardware access
  • → DFIR gets guaranteed hardware access; Cybersecurity gets repeated in-person lab support without requiring more classrooms
Estimated cost model: keep the virtual backbone for all 100 learners at approximately $25–35 per learner per month for cloud ranges, shared infrastructure, and platform costs = $2,500–3,500/month, or roughly $10,000–14,000 across a 4-month cohort. Maintain 25 DFIR physical kits at approximately $300–400 per kit = $7,500–10,000 one-time, reusable across future cohorts. Total estimated Cohort 1 infrastructure cost remains roughly $17,500–24,000, with later cohorts dropping substantially because hardware is already owned.

Operating implication: keep all groups synchronized by week, rotate physical attendance within each group instead of designating one group as remote, and publish the classroom / kit schedule before launch. The biggest requirement is not more space — it is tighter operational coordination, stronger hybrid facilitation, and artifact-based lab design that works equally well for in-room and online learners.

Staffing & Assessment Model

  • Core delivery model: 1 virtual concept session for all learners each week, plus hybrid lab delivery across 3 groups. Every group includes both in-person and online learners during lab days; no group is fully remote.
  • Wks 0–2 (Shared foundation): Lead instructor × 1 + support instructor × 1 + TA support for hybrid moderation and troubleshooting. Both instructors should be present for the Wk 2 Red/Blue debrief, which requires coordinated facilitation across tracks and modalities.
  • Wks 3–12 (Split tracks): Cybersecurity lead × 1 + DFIR lead × 1. Cybersecurity runs across three hybrid groups; DFIR runs as one smaller hybrid track with physical priority during hardware weeks. At least 3 TAs / lab mentors should be assigned consistently across the three groups.
  • Wks 13–16 (Converge): Both instructors jointly lead purple-team and capstone sessions, with TAs supporting breakout rooms, artifact review, hybrid participation, and presentation prep.
  • Hybrid facilitation requirement: one person must always own the room, and one must always own the online experience. Even when only one lead instructor is teaching, TA support must actively monitor chat, online questions, breakout issues, and submission logistics.
  • Guest sessions (Wks 5, 9, 13): Jordan SOC/DFIR practitioners, lunch-and-learn format. Both tracks attend — builds realistic job expectations and cross-track professional networks.

Instructional staffing recommendation:

  • Minimum: 2 instructors + 3 TAs / lab mentors for steady-state delivery
  • Preferred: 2 instructors + 4–5 TAs / lab mentors during Wks 3–12, especially for Cybersecurity labs and DFIR hardware sessions
  • Why: the constraint is not only content expertise — it is hybrid orchestration, debugging load, artifact review, and maintaining parity between in-room and online learners

Assessment framework:

  • Weekly readiness checks: both tracks, shared low-stakes rubric, completed by all learners regardless of physical or online participation mode
  • Track-specific lab rubrics: Wks 3–12, graded on technical accuracy, documentation quality, reproducibility, and appropriate use of AI audit logs
  • Hybrid parity rule: deliverables and grading standards are identical for in-person and online learners; the mode changes, not the expectations
  • Purple labs (Wks 13–14): graded equally on attack realism, detection quality, analysis quality, and cross-track communication
  • Capstone (Wks 15–16): graded on technical artifacts, final briefing quality, teamwork across red/DFIR roles, and AI governance compliance
  • TA workflow: TAs review readiness checks, support lab troubleshooting, verify artifact completeness, and escalate technical or integrity concerns to instructors

Risk Mitigation & Critical Flags

  • Evidence habits in Wk 1, not Wk 3: Chain-of-custody, hashing, and time-normalization are in the shared foundation. If DFIR learners miss this during an offense-heavy opening, the program's legal defensibility is at risk from the very first imaging lab.
  • "Write a detection" every red week: Non-negotiable. Red graduates who cannot think defensively are harder to place and more likely to cause harm in real engagements.
  • AI audit log from Week 1: Every AI-assisted output logged (model/prompt/version/variance). This is a hire-or-no-hire signal in AI-augmented SOCs by 2026.
  • Broadband risk in Amman: Offline VM image library downloaded at Week 0 is the fallback. No lab session should ever be blocked by a connectivity failure.
  • Physical kit schedule at Week 0: DFIR learners need guaranteed access in Wk 3 (imaging) and Wk 11 (mobile). Ambiguity here causes the highest-stakes labs to fail.
  • Honest learner framing: AI is automating Tier-1 triage. This program's emphasis on analysis, decision-making, and AI governance is the commercially correct response — communicate it as a feature, not a warning.
