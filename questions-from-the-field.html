Program fit

Who should apply, and what should they know first?

This section is for the first-pass questions: whether the program fits your background, whether you need prior tooling experience, and what kind of learner tends to succeed here.
Audience

Who this program is for

Who is this program built for? +

The primary audience is unemployed graduates from low-income families in Jordan with degrees in computer science, IT, engineering, or related technical fields. A strong fit looks like someone who is motivated, disciplined, technically curious, and willing to commit to an intensive schedule.

There is also a secondary audience: early-career professionals who want to pivot into cybersecurity or digital forensics roles but may not qualify for FSF funding.

Do I need professional experience before applying? +

No. The program is not built around prior industry experience. What matters more is technical curiosity, follow-through, structured problem-solving, and the ability to stay engaged in an intensive learning environment.

What kind of learner tends to do well here? +

The strongest learners are not necessarily the most experienced — they are the most disciplined. This is a good fit for people who can follow structured workflows, document their work, ask clear questions, and stay consistent over weeks, not just days.

Prerequisites

What you should already know

What prior knowledge or skills should candidates have before applying? +
  • Familiarity with operating systems such as Windows and/or Linux
  • Basic networking concepts like TCP/IP, DNS, and HTTP
  • Comfort with structured problem-solving
Do I need Bash or command-line experience already? +

Helpful, yes. Required, no. Command-line basics are covered in pre-work before the core program begins, so prior Bash fluency is not a gate to entry.

Do I need Git or GitHub before starting? +

No. Git workflows and GitHub are introduced during pre-work. Learners use GitHub throughout the program, but no prior experience is expected.

What learners experience

Why this is structured the way it is

These questions get at how the program actually feels: why it is practice-driven, why it is not just another online course, how the capstone works, and what a typical week demands.
Learning model

Practice before theory fades

If a learner could take any online course instead, why choose this program? +
  • Structured, supervised intensity rather than self-paced drift
  • Cohort-based accountability and real team dynamics
  • Purple teaming by design, with both tracks working together in the capstone
  • An FSF-funded pathway for eligible learners
  • Employment support through networking, portfolio guidance, and employer-facing preparation
  • Hybrid delivery, not a purely virtual experience
What does “practice-driven” actually mean? +

It means learners are not just hearing about tools, frameworks, or workflows — they are using them inside structured, evidence-safe scenarios that mirror operational environments.

  • Hands-on labs that reflect real workflows
  • Scenario-based exercises such as intrusions, phishing, malware, and insider-threat style cases
  • Structured reporting and documentation, not just button-clicking
  • MITRE ATT&CK-aligned reporting across the program
Can you give one concrete example of that practice-driven model? +

Yes. Early in the program, Cybersecurity learners run Nmap and Masscan scans against lab targets. In the same period, Digital Forensics learners analyze the Windows artifacts generated by those exact actions: event IDs, registry changes, and file creation. One side produces the activity. The other side investigates the evidence. That red/blue feedback loop carries through the program.

Time & structure

What a serious week looks like

How much time per week does the program require? +

Learners should expect a minimum of roughly 30–40 hours per week during the core program. That time includes live sessions, labs, assignments, English-language learning, soft-skills development, and self-study.

What does a typical week include? +
  • Live concept teaching
  • Applied labs
  • Assignments and structured practice
  • English-language learning
  • Soft-skills sessions
  • Peer interaction and self-study
Is this something I can casually fit around other major commitments? +

It should be approached as a serious training commitment. The program is built for learners who can show up consistently, stay on pace, and treat the work like a meaningful pre-employment pathway rather than a hobby course.

Best expectation: plan around the program, not around the hope that it will somehow fit without deliberate time management.
Capstone

How the final phase works

What does the capstone project look like? +

The capstone is a joint red/blue team exercise in the final phase of the program. Cybersecurity and Digital Forensics learners work in mixed teams around a realistic security engagement.

  • Cybersecurity learners plan and execute an attack campaign in a lab environment
  • Digital Forensics learners perform acquisition, build timelines, and analyze artifacts from that same scenario
  • Both sides document their work and map activity to MITRE ATT&CK where relevant
  • The work ends in a presentation to a CISO-style panel
What would a strong capstone include? +
  • A realistic attack chain with documented Rules of Engagement
  • Forensic imaging and validated chain of custody where appropriate
  • A unified timeline connecting offensive actions to defensive findings
  • An executive-level briefing backed by a technical appendix
  • Professional, reproducible documentation throughout

Tracks & careers

How the two specializations differ — and where they point

Specialization

Choosing and understanding the tracks

In plain language, what is the difference between the two specializations? +

Cybersecurity is the attacker’s mindset applied defensively: finding weaknesses, simulating attacks, and reporting what should be fixed before a real adversary exploits it.

Digital Forensics is the investigation side: figuring out what happened, preserving evidence, maintaining chain of custody, and producing defensible findings.

Together, both tracks reflect how strong security teams work in practice.

How do learners choose a specialization? +

Applicants are not asked to simply pick a track on the application form. Placement happens during onboarding based on interests, assessment results, and education-team judgment.

Should applicants ask themselves anything before track placement? +

Yes. Ask yourself whether you are more energized by finding weaknesses and simulating attacks, or by investigating what happened, preserving evidence, and building defensible findings. Both start from the same foundation, but the day-to-day mindset is different.

Are there industries or job titles that fit one specialization better than the other? +

Cybersecurity: Penetration testing firms, red team roles, security consultancies, tech companies, and MSSP-style environments.

Digital Forensics: Government-related work, law-enforcement support, banking and finance, large enterprises with SOC functions, insurance/legal cyber claims, and regulatory or compliance-heavy environments.

  • Run a full lab-based penetration test from recon through report delivery
  • Image a drive with validated hashes and defensible documentation

Support, funding & outcomes

What support exists, how funding works, and what learners leave with

These are the practical closing questions: what happens if a learner struggles, how the funding model works, what the program gives back at the end, and how to think about instructor credibility.
Support systems

What happens if a learner struggles?

What support exists if a learner falls behind? +
  • Weekly attendance dashboard review and follow-up
  • Weekly reflection review with follow-up
  • Weekly LMS progress review and intervention
  • First-line technical LMS support
  • Teaching Assistant office hours for group and individual support
Does the program just leave learners on their own once the workload gets hard? +

No. The program model is built around structured monitoring, intervention, and support. Struggle is expected. Silent drift is what the system is designed to catch.

Funding

How the FSF pathway works

What does the FSF funding cover, and who is eligible? +
  • No upfront payment for eligible learners
  • Repayment begins only after employment is secured
  • Repayment is capped at 10% of monthly salary
  • No guarantor required
  • Eligibility depends on FSF criteria
What if someone does not qualify for FSF funding? +

The program is also considering or supporting a self-pay pathway for early-career professionals and others outside the FSF eligibility rules.

Outcomes

What learners walk away with

  • A documented portfolio of lab work, reports, and exercises
  • A capstone project and final presentation
  • A structured, career-ready GitHub repository
  • A guided LinkedIn profile
  • Exposure to industry practitioners
  • A graduate title framed around Cybersecurity Analyst (SOC/IR) with specialization
Where does that portfolio live? +

The intended model is a structured portfolio presentation of lab work, reports, and capstone outputs, supported by GitHub repositories and professional positioning artifacts.

What are some examples of “cool” skills learners may actually build? +
  • Intercept live network traffic and inspect what is being transmitted
  • Recover deleted evidence through forensic imaging workflows
  • Crack a weak hashed password and explain why it fails so quickly
  • Analyze malware behavior in a sandboxed environment
  • Bypass a defense in a lab, then detect the same behavior from the blue side
  • Write a Python script that automates scanning and parses output into useful signal
