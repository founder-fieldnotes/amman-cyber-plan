Program fit

Who this is for, and what you should already know

Start here if you are asking the first practical questions: Is this meant for someone like me? Do I need prior experience? Do I already need Bash, Git, or GitHub?
Who is this program built for? −

The primary audience is unemployed graduates from low-income families in Jordan with degrees in computer science, IT, engineering, or related technical fields.

The best fit is someone motivated, self-directed, disciplined, and technically curious. Prior work experience is not the main filter. Readiness and follow-through matter more.

There is also a secondary audience: early-career professionals who want to pivot into cybersecurity or digital forensics roles but may not qualify for FSF funding.

What prior knowledge or skills should candidates have before applying? +
  • Familiarity with operating systems such as Windows and/or Linux
  • Basic networking concepts like TCP/IP, DNS, and HTTP
  • Comfort with structured problem-solving
Windows / Linux TCP/IP Problem-solving
Do I need Bash or command-line experience already? +

Helpful, yes. Required, no. Command-line basics are covered in pre-work before the core program begins, so prior Bash fluency is not a gate to entry.

Do I need Git or GitHub before starting? +

No. Git workflows and GitHub are introduced during pre-work. Learners use GitHub throughout the program, but no prior experience is expected.

What kind of learner tends to do well here? +

The strongest learners are not necessarily the most experienced. They are the most disciplined. This is a good fit for people who can document their work, follow a structured process, ask clear questions, and stay consistent over time.

Learning model

Why this is more than an online course

These are the questions behind most serious comparisons: what “practice-driven” means, why a cohort model matters, how the capstone works, and what the weekly commitment actually feels like.
One concrete example

The same activity, seen from both sides

Early in the program, Cybersecurity learners run Nmap and Masscan scans against lab targets. In the same part of the program, Digital Forensics learners analyze the artifacts those exact actions created: Windows events, registry writes, and file changes.

One side generates the activity. The other side investigates the evidence. That is what “practice-driven” means here: not isolated exercises, but connected operational thinking.

If a learner could take any online course instead, why choose this program? −
  • It is structured and supervised rather than self-paced
  • It is cohort-based, which creates accountability and real team dynamics
  • It uses purple teaming by design, with both tracks working together in the capstone
  • It includes an FSF-funded route for eligible learners
  • It includes employment-facing preparation such as networking, portfolios, GitHub, and LinkedIn positioning
  • It uses hybrid delivery rather than a purely virtual experience
What does “practice-driven” actually mean? +

It means learners are not just hearing about tools, frameworks, or workflows. They are using them inside structured, evidence-safe scenarios that mirror operational environments.

  • Hands-on labs that reflect real workflows
  • Scenario-based exercises such as intrusions, phishing, malware, and insider-threat style cases
  • Structured documentation and reporting, not just tool use
  • MITRE ATT&CK-aligned reporting throughout
How much time per week does the program require? +

Learners should expect a minimum of roughly 30–40 hours per week during the core program. That includes live sessions, labs, assignments, English-language learning, soft-skills development, and self-study.

What does a typical week include? +
  • Live concept teaching
  • Applied labs
  • Assignments and structured practice
  • English-language learning
  • Soft-skills sessions
  • Peer interaction and self-study
What does the capstone project look like? +

The capstone is a joint red/blue team exercise in the final phase of the program. Cybersecurity and Digital Forensics learners work in mixed teams around a realistic security engagement.

  • Cybersecurity learners plan and execute an attack campaign in a lab environment
  • Digital Forensics learners acquire, analyze, and build timelines from the same scenario
  • Both sides document their work and connect activity to frameworks and findings
  • The phase ends in a presentation to a CISO-style panel
Tracks & careers

How the specializations differ, and where they lead

This is where applicants usually want the simplest possible explanation: what each track actually does, how placement works, and what roles or industries each path aligns with.
Cybersecurity & Digital Forensics

Understanding the difference

In plain language, what is the difference between the two specializations? −

Cybersecurity is the attacker’s mindset applied defensively: finding weaknesses, simulating attacks, and reporting what should be fixed before a real adversary exploits it.

Digital Forensics is the investigation side: figuring out what happened, preserving evidence, maintaining chain of custody, and producing defensible findings.

Both tracks start from a shared foundation and eventually work together in the capstone.

How do learners choose a specialization? +

Applicants are not simply asked to pick a track on the form. Placement happens during onboarding based on interests, assessment results, and education-team judgment.

Are there job titles or industries that fit one specialization better than the other? +

Cybersecurity: penetration testing firms, red team roles, security consultancies, tech companies, and MSSP-style environments.

Digital Forensics: government-related work, banking and finance, large enterprises with SOC functions, insurance/legal cyber claims, and compliance-heavy environments.

What can this program’s graduates often do that a university-only graduate cannot do on Day 1? +
  • Run a full lab-based penetration test from recon through report delivery
  • Image a drive with validated hashes and defensible documentation
  • Triage alerts, parse logs, and escalate incidents in a SOC-style workflow
  • Script parts of their workflow in Python or Bash
  • Work across offense and defense in purple-team style collaboration
  • Write executive-ready and technical reports
  • Use AI tools with validation, auditability, and documented judgment
The difference is operational capability. The goal is not only to explain concepts, but to perform, document, and defend the work.
Support & outcomes

What support exists, how funding works, and what learners leave with

These are the most practical closing questions: what happens if someone struggles, how the funding model works, what the program gives back at the end, and how to think about credibility without overstating what is still being finalized.
Support systems

What happens if a learner struggles?

What support exists if a learner falls behind? −
  • Weekly attendance dashboard review and follow-up
  • Weekly reflection review with follow-up
  • Weekly LMS progress review and intervention
  • First-line technical LMS support
  • Teaching Assistant office hours for group and individual support
Does the program just leave learners on their own once the workload gets hard? +

No. The operating model is built around monitoring, intervention, and support. Struggle is expected. Silent drift is what the system is designed to catch.

Funding

How the FSF pathway works

What does the FSF funding cover, and who is eligible? −
  • No upfront payment for eligible learners
  • Repayment begins only after employment is secured
  • Repayment is capped at 10% of monthly salary
  • No guarantor required
  • Eligibility depends on FSF criteria
What if someone does not qualify for FSF funding? +

The program is also supporting or considering a self-pay path for early-career professionals and others outside the FSF eligibility rules.

What learners leave with

Proof, not just participation

The program is built so that learners leave with visible, career-relevant artifacts rather than only a completion claim.

  • A documented portfolio of lab work, reports, and exercises
  • A capstone project and final presentation
  • A structured, career-ready GitHub repository
  • A guided LinkedIn profile
  • Exposure to industry practitioners
  • A graduate title framed around Cybersecurity Analyst (SOC/IR) with specialization
Portfolio GitHub Capstone LinkedIn
Think of the outcome as evidence of capability. The point is not just to finish. The point is to leave with work you can show.
